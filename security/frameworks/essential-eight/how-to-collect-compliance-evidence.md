# How to Collect Compliance Evidence

---

## Overview

This guide explains how to collect, organise, and present compliance evidence for an Essential Eight assessment. It covers planning an evidence programme, gathering evidence for each of the eight controls, maintaining documentation standards, and preparing an audit package.

For maturity level requirements and detailed specifications, see the [Essential Eight Maturity Model Reference](reference-maturity-model.md).

For authoritative requirements, refer to the [ACSC Essential Eight Assessment Process Guide](https://www.cyber.gov.au/resources-business-and-government/essential-cybersecurity/essential-eight/essential-eight-assessment-process-guide).

---

## Before You Begin

This guide assumes you are:

- Responsible for demonstrating Essential Eight compliance to an internal governance body or an external assessor.
- Working towards a defined target maturity level (ML1, ML2, or ML3).
- Operating in an environment where the eight controls have at least partially been implemented.

If controls are not yet implemented, complete implementation before collecting compliance evidence. Refer to [How to Implement Essential Eight Controls in Azure](how-to-implement-e8-controls.md) for implementation guidance.

---

## Key Principles

Before collecting any evidence, internalise these principles. They apply to every control and every maturity level.

**Completeness.** Collect evidence for every requirement at your target maturity level. Partial evidence packages invite assessor questions and risk non-compliance findings.

**Accuracy.** Evidence must reflect the actual configuration and behaviour of your environment at the time of collection. Do not present evidence from a test or staging environment as representative of production.

**Timeliness.** Evidence has a currency requirement. Stale evidence — collected months before an assessment — may not satisfy an assessor. Maintain a regular collection schedule.

**Authenticity.** Evidence must be traceable to its source. Maintain a chain of custody: record who collected each artefact, when, and from which system.

**Relevance.** Every artefact you collect should map directly to an ACSC requirement. Avoid accumulating large volumes of tangentially related material that obscures the evidence package.

## Step 1 — Map Requirements to Evidence Types

Before collecting anything, produce a requirements-to-evidence mapping for your target maturity level. This mapping becomes the backbone of your evidence programme.

For each Essential Eight control at your target maturity level:

1. Read the ACSC requirement statement from the [Essential Eight Maturity Model](https://www.cyber.gov.au/resources-business-and-government/essential-cybersecurity/essential-eight/essential-eight-maturity-model).
2. Identify what observable state the requirement demands (a configuration value, a process outcome, a log record, or a policy).
3. Determine which evidence type can demonstrate that state. Common evidence types are:

| Evidence Type | Description | Examples |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Configuration export | A machine-readable or human-readable export of a policy or setting | Policy XML, JSON exports, GPO HTML reports |
| Configuration screenshot | A timestamped screenshot of a setting in its management console | Browser hardening settings, MFA policy view |
| Audit log extract | A filtered extract from an audit or event log confirming an action occurred | Sign-in logs, application block events, patch installation records |
| Compliance report | A structured report generated by a management platform showing adherence status | Patch compliance dashboards, MFA enrolment reports, backup job summaries |
| Policy document | An approved organisational policy or procedure | Backup policy, privileged access policy, exception register |
| Test result | Documented outcome of a functional test confirming a control operates as intended | Application block test, macro block test, restoration test |
| Access review record | Documented review of account membership or privilege assignments | Privileged account register, quarterly access review sign-off |

4. Assign a collection frequency (daily, weekly, monthly, quarterly) and an owner for each evidence item.
5. Record the mapping in a central evidence register — a spreadsheet or a documented table is sufficient.

**Maturity level evidence depth:**

| Maturity Level | Evidence Depth | Minimum Collection Frequency | Recommended Retention |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| ML1 | Basic configuration evidence and policy documents | Monthly | 12 months |
| ML2 | Automated compliance reports and audit log evidence | Weekly | 24 months |
| ML3 | Continuous monitoring outputs and real-time dashboard exports | Daily | 36 months |

## Step 2 — Establish an Evidence Repository

An organised repository is essential. Assessors and auditors expect to locate evidence quickly. Disorganised repositories create delays and undermine confidence in your compliance programme.

Recommended folder structure:

```
Evidence-Repository/
├── Application-Control/
│   ├── ML1/
│   │   ├── Configuration/
│   │   ├── Logs/
│   │   ├── Reports/
│   │   └── Screenshots/
│   ├── ML2/
│   └── ML3/
├── Patch-Applications/
├── Configure-Office-Macros/
├── User-Application-Hardening/
├── Restrict-Administrative-Privileges/
├── Patch-Operating-Systems/
├── Multi-Factor-Authentication/
├── Regular-Backups/
├── Exception-Register/
│   ├── Active-Exceptions/
│   └── Closed-Exceptions/
└── Compliance-Reports/
    ├── Monthly/
    ├── Quarterly/
    └── Annual/
```

Apply these storage controls to the repository:

- **Read-only for historical artefacts.** Once evidence for a past collection period is stored, it should not be modifiable. This preserves authenticity.
- **Access controls.** Limit write access to authorised collectors. Limit read access to the compliance team and authorised assessors.
- **Backup the repository.** Apply the same backup and retention practices to the evidence repository that you apply to other critical data.
- **Audit logging.** Enable access and modification logging on the repository store.

## Step 3 — Collect Evidence by Control

This section lists the minimum evidence required at each maturity level for each Essential Eight control. Collect all items applicable to your target maturity level. Higher maturity levels are additive — ML3 requires all ML1 and ML2 evidence plus the ML3-specific items.

### Control 1 — Application Control

**ML1 minimum:**

- [ ] Application control policy export (XML or equivalent) showing approved applications and rules
- [ ] Policy enforcement mode confirmation (enforce, not audit-only)
- [ ] List of approved applications
- [ ] Test result: an unapproved application was blocked
- [ ] Test result: an approved application executed successfully
- [ ] Event log extract confirming block events occurred (e.g., application block events from the enforcement engine)

**ML2 additions:**

- [ ] Server application control policies (separate from workstation policies)
- [ ] Script control policy covering relevant scripting engines
- [ ] Digital signature validation configuration

**ML3 additions:**

- [ ] Coverage report demonstrating 100% of workstations and servers are enrolled
- [ ] Evidence of zero standing exemptions
- [ ] Driver control policy configuration

**Collection frequency:** Monthly for configuration exports; event log extracts on demand or weekly at ML2+.

---

### Control 2 — Patch Applications

**ML1 minimum:**

- [ ] Application inventory report listing software names, versions, and vendors across managed endpoints
- [ ] Vulnerability scan results identifying unpatched applications
- [ ] Patch deployment report showing patch status per system
- [ ] Exception register for any systems with outstanding critical patches, including documented approval and remediation timeline
- [ ] Key metrics: percentage of internet-facing applications patched within 30 days of release; number of outstanding critical vulnerabilities

**ML2 additions:**

- [ ] Automated patch deployment configuration evidence
- [ ] Evidence of internet-facing application patches applied within two weeks of an exploited vulnerability disclosure
- [ ] Test environment validation results prior to production deployment

**ML3 additions:**

- [ ] Evidence of patches applied within 48 hours of an extreme risk (CVSS 9.0+) vulnerability disclosure
- [ ] Emergency patching procedure documentation

**Collection frequency:** Monthly for inventory and compliance reports; on-demand for exploited vulnerability patching evidence.

---

### Control 3 — Configure Microsoft Office Macro Settings

**ML1 minimum:**

- [ ] Policy configuration export showing macro blocking settings for all Office applications
- [ ] Trusted locations documentation (or confirmation that no trusted locations are configured)
- [ ] Digital signature requirements documentation
- [ ] Test result: a macro sourced from the internet was blocked
- [ ] Test result: a signed macro from a trusted publisher executed successfully

**ML2 additions:**

- [ ] Attack Surface Reduction rule configuration for blocking Win32 API calls from Office macros
- [ ] Macro logging and monitoring configuration
- [ ] Evidence of macro event logs being collected or forwarded

**ML3 additions:**

- [ ] Sandboxed macro execution environment configuration (if applicable)
- [ ] Advanced threat protection integration evidence showing macro-based threats are being detected

**Collection frequency:** Monthly.

---

### Control 4 — User Application Hardening

**ML1 minimum:**

- [ ] Web browser hardening policy export (covering web advertisement blocking, Java blocking for internet content, and extension management)
- [ ] Evidence that Flash content is blocked or that Flash is not installed
- [ ] Evidence that Java is blocked for internet-sourced content
- [ ] Test result: an unauthorised browser extension was blocked from installation
- [ ] Test result: a Flash or Java applet was blocked when sourced from the internet

**ML2 additions:**

- [ ] DNS-level or network-level filtering configuration evidence
- [ ] Enhanced tracking prevention or equivalent configuration

**ML3 additions:**

- [ ] Advanced browser hardening configuration (e.g., site isolation, process isolation)

**Collection frequency:** Monthly.

---

### Control 5 — Restrict Administrative Privileges

**ML1 minimum:**

- [ ] Privileged account inventory (domain and local administrator accounts)
- [ ] Local administrator group membership report for workstations and servers
- [ ] Evidence that standard users do not hold local administrator rights on their primary workstations
- [ ] Privileged account usage logs showing privileged accounts are used only for administrative tasks
- [ ] Quarterly access review records with sign-off

**ML2 additions:**

- [ ] Just-in-time (JIT) administration configuration evidence
- [ ] Privileged access workstation (PAW) inventory and usage evidence
- [ ] Privileged Identity Management (PIM) or equivalent configuration

**ML3 additions:**

- [ ] Evidence that all privileged operations are conducted from PAWs
- [ ] Evidence of zero standing privileges (all privileged access is time-bound)
- [ ] Privileged session monitoring logs

**Collection frequency:** Monthly for account and usage reports; quarterly for access reviews.

---

### Control 6 — Patch Operating Systems

**ML1 minimum:**

- [ ] Operating system inventory (system names, OS versions, build numbers)
- [ ] Vulnerability scan results (OS-specific)
- [ ] OS patch deployment report showing patch status per system
- [ ] Exception documentation for systems with outstanding OS patches, including approval and remediation timeline
- [ ] Key metrics: percentage of systems patched within one month of release; number of end-of-support operating systems

**ML2 additions:**

- [ ] Evidence of OS patches for exploited vulnerabilities applied within two weeks of disclosure
- [ ] Automated patch deployment configuration evidence
- [ ] Test environment validation results

**ML3 additions:**

- [ ] Evidence of OS patches for extreme risk vulnerabilities applied within 48 hours of disclosure
- [ ] Emergency OS patching procedure documentation

**Collection frequency:** Monthly.

---

### Control 7 — Multi-Factor Authentication

**ML1 minimum:**

- [ ] MFA enrolment report for all internet-facing services, showing enrolment status per user
- [ ] Confirmation of 100% MFA enrolment for all privileged accounts
- [ ] MFA method distribution (authenticator app, hardware token, SMS — note: SMS is not phishing-resistant)
- [ ] Conditional Access or equivalent policy configuration showing MFA is enforced for relevant services
- [ ] Sign-in log extract confirming MFA challenges are occurring

**ML2 additions:**

- [ ] Phishing-resistant MFA evidence (FIDO2 security keys, certificate-based authentication, or equivalent)
- [ ] Evidence of MFA enforcement for all users, not only privileged accounts
- [ ] Legacy authentication blocking configuration evidence

**ML3 additions:**

- [ ] Evidence of phishing-resistant MFA universally enforced across all users and all services
- [ ] Hardware security key deployment and usage evidence
- [ ] Passwordless authentication adoption metrics

**Collection frequency:** Weekly for enrolment and sign-in reports.

---

### Control 8 — Regular Backups

**ML1 minimum:**

- [ ] Backup schedule configuration showing frequency (at minimum daily for critical data)
- [ ] Backup scope documentation listing which systems and data are included
- [ ] Retention policy documentation (minimum three months)
- [ ] Evidence of disconnected or offline backup copies
- [ ] Backup job status report showing successful completion over the past 30 days
- [ ] Restoration test documentation (test date, system restored, outcome)

**ML2 additions:**

- [ ] Automated restoration testing configuration and test results
- [ ] Immutable backup configuration evidence
- [ ] Backup encryption confirmation
- [ ] Multi-site or off-site replication evidence

**ML3 additions:**

- [ ] Continuous or near-continuous backup verification evidence
- [ ] Air-gapped backup configuration and access control evidence
- [ ] Disaster recovery drill documentation (scenario, outcome, gaps identified)

**Collection frequency:** Daily for backup job status; quarterly for restoration tests; monthly for configuration exports.

## Step 4 — Apply Documentation Standards

Consistent documentation standards ensure evidence remains usable over time and survives personnel changes.

### File Naming Convention

Use a consistent naming pattern for all evidence artefacts:

```
{ControlNumber}-{MaturityLevel}-{EvidenceType}-{YYYY-MM-DD}.{ext}
```

Examples:

- `01-ML1-AppControl-Policy-2026-03-23.xml`
- `07-ML2-MFA-Enrolment-Report-2026-03-23.csv`
- `08-ML3-Backup-Restoration-Test-2026-03-23.pdf`

### Evidence Metadata

For each artefact, create an accompanying metadata record. A JSON sidecar file or a row in your evidence register is sufficient:

```json
{
  "evidenceID": "E8-01-ML1-REQ1-001",
  "control": "Application-Control",
  "requirement": "ML1",
  "evidenceType": "Configuration Export",
  "collectionDate": "2026-03-23T14:30:00+10:00",
  "collectedBy": "compliance-officer@organisation.com.au",
  "collectionMethod": "Manual export from management console",
  "fileName": "01-ML1-AppControl-Policy-2026-03-23.xml",
  "retentionDate": "2027-03-23",
  "notes": "Monthly evidence collection — March 2026"
}
```

### Central Evidence Register

Maintain a central evidence register that indexes every artefact:

| Evidence ID | Control | Maturity Level | Evidence Type | Collection Date | Collected By | File Location | Status |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| E8-01-001 | Application Control | ML1 | Config Export | 2026-03-23 | compliance@org | /01/ML1/Config/ | Valid |
| E8-07-042 | MFA | ML2 | Enrolment Report | 2026-03-23 | compliance@org | /07/ML2/Reports/ | Valid |

## Step 5 — Establish a Collection Schedule

Consistency is more important than volume. A predictable, automated schedule is more defensible to an assessor than an ad-hoc collection performed immediately before an audit.

| Evidence Category | Recommended Frequency | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Configuration exports | Monthly | All eight controls |
| Vulnerability scan results | Monthly | Controls 2 and 6 |
| MFA enrolment status | Weekly | Control 7 |
| Patch compliance reports | Weekly | Controls 2 and 6 |
| Backup job status | Daily | Control 8 |
| Privileged account usage logs | Weekly at ML2+, monthly at ML1 | Control 5 |
| Policy documentation reviews | Quarterly | All controls |
| Restoration tests | Quarterly | Control 8 |
| Access reviews | Quarterly | Control 5 |

Where your environment supports it, automate time-sensitive collections (backup job status, MFA enrolment, patch compliance) using your management platform's reporting or export capabilities. Automation reduces manual effort, improves consistency, and ensures collection does not lapse.

---

## Step 6 — Manage Exceptions

Not all requirements will be met at the time of evidence collection. Exceptions must be documented rather than omitted. An undocumented gap in evidence is a compliance finding; a documented and managed exception with compensating controls may not be.

For each exception, record:

- **Exception description.** The specific requirement that cannot currently be met.
- **Affected systems or scope.** Which systems, user populations, or services are within scope of the exception.
- **Risk assessment.** The risk introduced by not meeting the requirement.
- **Compensating controls.** What mitigating measures are in place to reduce that risk.
- **Approval.** Who approved the exception and when.
- **Remediation plan.** What work is planned to close the exception, and by when.

Maintain an Exception Register as a formal document within the evidence repository. Review it quarterly and update it as exceptions are opened, approved, and closed.

---

## Step 7 — Prepare an Audit Package

When an assessment is scheduled, assemble a structured audit package from the evidence repository. A well-structured package reduces the time an assessor needs to locate evidence and signals a mature compliance programme.

Recommended audit package structure:

```
Audit-Package-{YYYY}-{Quarter}/
├── Executive-Summary.pdf
├── Evidence-Index.csv
├── Exception-Register.pdf
├── Application-Control/
│   ├── ML1-Evidence/
│   ├── ML2-Evidence/
│   └── Summary-Report.pdf
├── Patch-Applications/
├── Configure-Office-Macros/
├── User-Application-Hardening/
├── Restrict-Administrative-Privileges/
├── Patch-Operating-Systems/
├── Multi-Factor-Authentication/
└── Regular-Backups/
```

**Pre-audit timeline:**

| Timeframe | Actions |
| --- | --- |
| 3 months before | Review requirements against current evidence programme; identify and remediate gaps; confirm collection schedules are running |
| 1 month before | Run a full evidence collection pass; organise the repository; prepare summary reports; document all exceptions |
| 1 week before | Final evidence collection; verify all timestamps are current; prepare the audit package; brief the team presenting evidence |

## Common Pitfalls

**Incomplete evidence.** Use the per-control checklists in Step 3 as a pre-assessment completeness check. Every unchecked item is a potential finding.

**Stale evidence.** Evidence collected months before an assessment may not demonstrate current compliance. Assessors typically expect evidence that reflects the state of the environment at or near the assessment date. Maintain regular collection schedules rather than collecting on demand.

**Disorganised repository.** If you cannot locate a specific artefact within two minutes, neither can an assessor. Apply the folder structure and naming conventions in Steps 2 and 4 from the outset — retrofitting structure is time-consuming.

**No chain of custody.** If an assessor asks how evidence was collected, you must be able to answer: who collected it, from which system, by which method, and when. Use metadata records and your evidence register to maintain this chain.

**Undocumented exceptions.** An assessor who finds an unmet requirement with no corresponding exception record will treat it as an unmanaged gap. Document every known exception regardless of how minor it appears.

**Mixed-environment evidence.** Ensure evidence is drawn from production systems. Test or staging environment evidence does not demonstrate production compliance.
